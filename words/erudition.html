erudition

Dictionary

Definitions

  • noun
    • Deep, extensive learning. synonym: knowledge.
    • Learning; scholarship; knowledge gained by study or from books and instruction; particularly, learning in literature, history, antiquities, and languages, as distinct from knowledge of the mathematical and physical sciences.
    • The act of instructing; the result of thorough instruction; the state of being erudite or learned; the acquisitions gained by extensive reading or study; particularly, learning in literature or criticism, as distinct from the sciences; scholarship.
    • Profound knowledge, especially that based on learning and scholarship.

Origin, Etymologies

From Latin eruditio ("an instructing, learning, erudition"), from erudire ("to instruct, educate, cultivate", literally "free from rudeness"), from e ("out") + rudis ("rude").

The word "erudition" comes from the Latin eruditio, meaning "learning," which is a noun from the verb erudire, meaning "to instruct" or "to educate". This Latin verb is a compound of e- ("out") and rudis ("rude," "unskilled," or "rough"), so erudire literally meant "to free from rudeness". Therefore, erudition is the result of being "taken out of a rough or uncultivated state" through a process of education and learning. 

  • Latin roots: erudire = e- ("out") + rudis ("rough," "unskilled").
  • Literal meaning: "To free from rudeness" or "to remove coarseness".
  • Figurative meaning: The process of educating or instructing someone, leading them from a state of being "rough" or unlearned to one of polished and extensive knowledge.
  • Modern usage: The noun "erudition" refers to the deep, scholarly knowledge that results from this process.
